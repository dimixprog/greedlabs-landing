**Beyond submitting an application, a centralized exchange typically requires legal and KYB documentation, credible tokenomics, a security audit, a market making commitment, and evidence of real community and volume.** This is the requirements checklist to prepare against; for the step-by-step application process, see [How to Get Listed on a CEX](/blog/how-to-get-listed-on-cex).

## Legal and entity

Exchanges run **KYB (know-your-business)** on the project: a legal entity, jurisdiction details, and the people behind it. Weak or opaque legal standing is a common rejection reason.

## Tokenomics and documentation

Clear, credible **tokenomics** — supply, allocations, vesting — plus documentation (whitepaper, contract details). Distribution matters too; see [Token Holder Distribution](/blog/token-holder-distribution).

## Security

A **security audit** of the token contract from a recognized firm, and no unresolved critical issues. Exchanges don't want to list a contract that could embarrass them.

## Liquidity and market making

Almost every listing comes with a **market making commitment** — maintaining spread, depth, and uptime so the market works after launch. Budget for it alongside the listing fee; see [How Much Does a Crypto Market Maker Cost?](/blog/crypto-market-maker-cost) and check terms in the [glossary](/blog/market-making-glossary).

## Community and real traction

Evidence of a **genuine community and organic activity**. Fabricated numbers backfire — exchanges detect them. See [How to Detect Fake Volume](/blog/how-to-detect-fake-volume) for what they're screening against.

## Fees and tier

Costs and the bar both rise with exchange size — see [Tier-1 vs. Tier-2 Exchange Listings](/blog/tier-1-vs-tier-2-exchange-listings).

Meet these requirements and the application process gets far smoother — and the liquidity commitment at the center of them is exactly what we provide.
